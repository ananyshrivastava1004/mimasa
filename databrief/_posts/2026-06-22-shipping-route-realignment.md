Persistent disruptions in key maritime chokepoints have pushed a meaningful share of global container traffic onto longer, costlier routes over the past two years.

## The detour becomes permanent

What began as a temporary rerouting around trouble spots has, for many carriers, become the new normal. Longer transit times are being priced into contracts rather than treated as a one-off disruption.

## Second-order effects

The extra transit time has pulled forward demand for regional manufacturing hubs closer to end markets, accelerating a trend some analysts have called "nearshoring 2.0."
