Hyperscale cloud providers have shifted from a growth story to an infrastructure story. Capital expenditure that once went toward general-purpose servers is now overwhelmingly earmarked for AI-specific compute: custom silicon, liquid-cooled racks, and the power contracts needed to run them.

## Where the money is actually going

Roughly three-quarters of new capex is now tied directly to AI workloads, split across three buckets: custom accelerator chips, networking fabric to link thousands of chips together, and — increasingly — the electricity generation and transmission needed to keep it all running.

## Power is the new bottleneck

Chip supply, once the binding constraint, has eased somewhat. The harder problem now is power. Several major data center campuses have been delayed not by hardware availability but by the multi-year wait for grid interconnection, pushing some operators to sign direct deals with nuclear and natural gas plants.

## What it means going forward

Expect the geography of AI infrastructure to keep shifting toward regions with cheap, abundant power and favorable regulation, even where that means longer network latency to end users. The buildout is no longer just a technology story — it is increasingly an energy and real estate one.
